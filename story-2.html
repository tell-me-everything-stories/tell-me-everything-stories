The Neighbor Who Called Code Enforcement on Us Every Summer Finally Got a Visit Herself

5 min read · Karma

For six summers in a row, our neighbor Carol called the county on us. The first year, it was our fence — two inches over the property line, according to her measurements, though the survey said otherwise. The second year, it was our above-ground pool, which apparently violated a setback rule she'd looked up specifically to use against us. Then it was our shed, our trampoline, the height of our hedge, and one memorable summer, the "excessive noise" of our kids playing in the yard at four in the afternoon.

Every single complaint got investigated. Every single one got dismissed, eventually, after weeks of paperwork and a county inspector standing in our driveway with a tape measure while Carol watched from her porch with her arms crossed.

We tried being friendly. We brought over tomatoes from the garden. We tried, once, just asking her directly if something was bothering her. She told us, very politely, that she was "just looking out for the neighborhood's standards." The neighborhood, as far as we could tell, consisted of eleven houses, and we were the only ones she seemed concerned about.

This past spring, Carol decided to build a detached garage in her backyard. A big one — big enough for an RV, from what we could see of the plans posted in her yard.

Here's the thing about small towns: everybody talks. And it turned out that Carol's new garage was about three feet closer to our shared property line than the setback rules allowed. The same setback rules, as it happens, that she'd quoted to the county about our shed five years earlier, chapter and verse, in an email she'd actually cc'd to half the street.

We didn't even have to do anything. One of our other neighbors, Tom, who'd been on the receiving end of his own Carol-complaint a few years back about his basketball hoop, was the one who walked over with a tape measure of his own, took a few pictures, and filed the report. He told us afterward, with a completely straight face, that he was "just looking out for the neighborhood's standards."

Carol had to halt construction for almost two months while the county sorted it out. In the end she had to redraw the plans and lose about four feet of garage space. We heard she was furious — convinced someone had it out for her, convinced it was "political."

We never said a word to her about it. We didn't have to. For the first time in six years, this past summer was quiet. No inspectors in our driveway, no certified letters in our mailbox, nothing. Just two families on the same street, finally minding their own business, the way it probably should have been all along.

I won't pretend I didn't smile a little when I saw the dumpster show up in her driveway for the leftover lumber. But mostly, I was just relieved. Six years of feeling watched, judged, and reported on, and it ended not with a confrontation, but with a tape measure — the same one, more or less, she'd used on us so many times before.

Funny how that works out.
