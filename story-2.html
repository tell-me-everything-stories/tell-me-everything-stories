The Neighbor Who Called Code Enforcement on Us Every Summer Finally Got a Visit Herself

Episode 1 · 16 min read · Neighbors · Karma · Justice

Ad space

The first time Carol Henderson called code enforcement on us, my husband Ray laughed. He actually laughed — stood at the kitchen window watching the county inspector measure our fence with a tape measure and shook his head and said, "You've got to be kidding me." We had just moved into the house eight months earlier. We were still in the stage of homeownership where everything feels like an adventure, where even a leaky faucet is something you can feel good about fixing. We had not yet learned that some neighbors treat a property line the way certain people treat a personal slight — as something that must be addressed, repeatedly, regardless of whether it affects them in any practical way.

Carol Henderson lived in the house directly to our left, a blue colonial with white shutters that she maintained with the focused intensity of someone for whom the house was not a home so much as a statement. Her lawn was edged with a precision that suggested a level of commitment I genuinely respected, even later, when I had every reason not to respect anything about her. Her flower beds were mulched in late April every year and again in early October. Her gutters were cleaned twice annually. She had lived on the street for nineteen years before we arrived and had, by all accounts, been a model neighbor to everyone during that time.

What changed when we moved in, I was never entirely able to determine. We introduced ourselves the first week. We brought over a tin of cookies that she accepted politely. We smiled and waved. We followed the HOA rules. We did nothing, as far as I could identify, to offend her.

And yet.

The fence complaint, that first year, was about two inches. Carol had measured from what she believed was the property line and determined that two inches of our fence post were on her side of it. The county inspector measured from the official survey markers and determined that the fence was, in fact, entirely within our property. He was apologetic and slightly embarrassed. He told us these things happen and wished us a good afternoon. Carol watched from her porch the entire time, arms crossed, expression unreadable.

The fence issue was, I thought, resolved. I was naive.

The following summer, we installed an above-ground pool in the backyard. Nothing elaborate — a round, four-foot-deep pool that our two kids could use during the months when the heat made the backyard otherwise uninhabitable. We checked the HOA rules. We verified the setback requirements. We were, as far as we could determine, entirely within our rights.

Three weeks after the pool went in, we received a certified letter from the county. A complaint had been filed. The pool, according to the complaint, violated a setback requirement that applied to permanent structures. The pool was not a permanent structure — it had no foundation, could be disassembled, and was explicitly in a category exempt from the regulation in question. The inspector who came out confirmed this. He made a note in his records. He told us the complaint had been anonymous but that we should know we were in full compliance.

Ray and I looked at each other across the backyard after the inspector left.

"Carol," Ray said.

"Carol," I agreed.

We could not prove it. We had no way to prove it. But there is a particular kind of certainty that comes from pattern recognition, and the pattern was becoming clear.

Year three brought a complaint about our storage shed — too close to the property line, according to the complaint. It wasn't. Year four was about the height of our hedge along the back fence, which was allegedly in violation of a sight-line ordinance that, upon investigation, turned out to apply only to properties within fifty feet of a designated roadway. We were not within fifty feet of a designated roadway. Year five was about noise — a formal complaint, filed with the county, alleging that our children were creating "excessive and disruptive noise" during daytime hours. The children in question were eight and eleven years old and were playing in our backyard on a Saturday afternoon.

Each complaint was investigated. Each complaint was dismissed. Each investigation required us to take time off work, or rearrange our schedules, or stand in our own backyard explaining ourselves to county employees who were, without exception, apologetic and slightly uncomfortable. After the third investigation, one inspector told me, off the record, that our address had become something of a running joke in the department. "You're the most-investigated compliant property in the county," he said, which I think was intended to be reassuring.

It was not reassuring.

We tried, in that fifth year, to address it directly. Ray knocked on Carol's door on a Sunday morning. He was calm. He had rehearsed. He told her that we were aware she had concerns about our property and that we would genuinely like to understand what those concerns were so we could address them if possible. He said we valued being good neighbors and wanted to find a way to coexist peacefully.

Carol listened to this entire speech with her arms crossed and her expression set. When Ray finished, she said, very pleasantly, that she had no idea what he was referring to, that she had simply been exercising her right as a citizen to report potential code violations, and that if our property was in compliance then we had nothing to worry about.

Ray came home and sat at the kitchen table and was quiet for a while.

"She's not going to stop," he said.

"No," I agreed. "She's not."

Year six, which was this past spring, Carol decided to build a detached garage in her backyard. Not a modest garage — a large one, built to accommodate an RV she had apparently purchased and needed somewhere to store. The plans appeared in her backyard one March morning, staked out with little orange flags, and our neighbor across the street, a retired contractor named Tom, walked over to look at them from the sidewalk and got a very particular expression on his face.

Tom had his own history with Carol. Three years earlier, she had filed a complaint about the basketball hoop in his driveway, which she claimed was a permanent structure requiring a permit. It was a portable basketball hoop. The kind with a base you fill with sand. Tom had moved it to his backyard to resolve the issue and had not forgotten.

Tom walked back across the street, went into his garage, and came back out with a tape measure.

I watched this from my kitchen window. I watched Tom measure from the orange flags to what I knew was the property line between Carol's lot and ours — the same line she had spent six years scrutinizing with such devoted attention. I watched him measure twice. I watched him take photographs. I watched his expression, which had started as curious and settled into something that looked very much like satisfaction.

He walked over to our house and rang the doorbell.

"You're going to want to see this," he said.

The proposed garage was three feet closer to our shared property line than the county setback ordinance permitted. The same setback ordinance that Carol had quoted, precisely and by regulation number, in her complaint about our storage shed in year three. She had included the regulation number in her written complaint. Tom still had the copy he had received from the county.

I want to be clear about something: I did not file the complaint. I thought about it. I will not pretend I didn't think about it, and think about it with considerable pleasure. But Ray and I talked about it, and we agreed that we did not want to become the kind of people who file complaints against their neighbors, even neighbors who had spent six years filing complaints against us. We had spent six years being investigated and exonerated, and we had not lost ourselves in it, and we were not going to lose ourselves now.

Tom, however, had made no such decision.

Tom filed the complaint on a Tuesday. He told me later that he had been straightforward in his report — he provided the measurements, the photographs, the regulation number, and a note that the complainant was the same property owner who had cited this specific regulation in a complaint against a neighbor three years prior. He said he felt it was relevant context.

The county agreed that it was relevant context.

Construction on Carol's garage halted two weeks later, while the county reviewed the complaint. The review took almost two months. During those two months, the construction equipment sat idle in her backyard, which I admit I found satisfying every single morning when I looked out the kitchen window.

The resolution required Carol to revise her plans and lose approximately four feet of the garage's intended footprint — enough that the RV she had purchased would not, as it turned out, fit inside it. We heard about the RV situation through the informal neighborhood communication network that operates in every suburb, the one made up of front-yard conversations and school pickup line exchanges and the particular kind of information that travels without anyone quite knowing how.

We also heard that Carol was furious. That she had accused someone — she didn't know who, she told people — of having it out for her. Of targeting her. Of being vindictive.

Ray and I did not say anything to Carol about any of it. We did not knock on her door. We did not acknowledge it in any way. We simply went on living our lives, as we had been doing for six years, in our house, in compliance with every applicable regulation, minding our own business.

This past summer was the first summer in six years that we did not receive a certified letter or an inspector in our driveway. The kids swam in the pool. We had people over for a barbecue in July. The hedge grew approximately four inches taller than it had been the year before.

Carol was outside the day of the barbecue, working in her front flower beds, and we waved at her from the driveway, and she waved back, and it was the most peaceful interaction we had ever had.

I don't think she's done. I know enough about how this kind of thing works to understand that people who need to control their environment don't simply stop needing to control it. But something shifted, and the summer was quiet, and my kids played in the backyard on Saturday afternoons without me holding my breath waiting for someone to tell me that joy was a code violation.

Tom, for what it's worth, got a very nice bottle of whiskey from us at Christmas. He said it was the best he'd ever tasted. We said we'd get him another one next year.
